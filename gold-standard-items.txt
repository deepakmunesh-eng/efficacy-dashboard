Part 3: Justification


The objective of this item is to ensure that the learners understand what a polygon is. It is broken into smaller widgets in a step by step with guided discovery and helps the student arrive at what a polygon is and also what is not a polygon from multiple examples and non examples. 
It begins with a prior knowledge of open and closed shapes first. Then it moves to only closed shapes and guides learners to categorise shapes that are made up of only straight lines, straight and curved lines and only curved lines. Then it defines what a polygon is and has built in CFUs to check if the student understands if a shape is a polygon or not with reasons. It ends with a final independent task to check which is a polygon. 


Part 4: A not so good approach

A closed shape that is made up of only straight lines is called a polygon.
	



Here is an example of a polygon.


  







The above shape is a polygon because it is a [closed*, open] shape and is made up of [only straight*, only curved, straight and curved] lines.

Part 5: Justification


This is not a great approach because of the following reasons.
1. It does not capture the prior knowledge of open and closed shapes and identification of straight and curved lines. Considering it is only grade 3 and students need time to develop geometric thinking, this scaffolding is needed.
2. This item does not have any guided task to dissect examples and non examples of polygons. 




Example 2: Grade 3: Number System: Identify the Place Value of a 4 Digit Number


[W02]
[FM]
[Instructor Stimulus: Instruction]




Place Value is the value of a digit at a given place.
	

Here is the number 2364.


  







Let’s see how we find the place value of each digit.
The one’s digit is 4. So, its place value  is 4 ones or 4.
The ten’s digit is 6. So, its place value is 6 tens or 60.
The hundred’s digit is 3. So, its place value is 3 hundreds or [300]
The thousand’s digit is 2. So, its place value is 2 thousands or [2000].


Hint 1: Place value tells us how much a digit is worth based on where it sits in the number.
Hint 2: For example, if 4 is in the tens place, it represents 4 tens, which is the same as 40.
Teacher Tips:
* What is 3 hundreds equal to? [300]
* What is 2 thousands equal to? [2000]
Solution: 
Answers: 300, 2000
For the number 2364:
* 3 in hundreds place represents 3 hundreds, which equals 300
* 2 in thousands place represents 2 thousands, which equals 2000
Acknowledgement:
What do 2 cubes represent in the number 2364?
How do you find the place value of the digit 2 in 2364?


Sample Answer:
The two cubes show the number 2000.
Place value equals the digit multiplied by the value of its place.
As 2 is in the thousands place in 2364, its place value is 2 x 1000 which is 2000.

[FM]

Hint 1: Count the blocks in each group to determine the digit in each place.
Hint 2: Each hundreds block represents 100, and each thousands block represents 1000.
Teacher Tips:

* Place value of digit in thousands place: 2 thousands = 2000

Part 3: Justification


In this item the concept moves from known to unknown and from concrete to abstract, first with models. Also observe that students already know about place value of 2 digit numbers and the first part of the item shows how to find place value. In the second part, there is a direct question with models to find the place value, followed by abstract question on place value and place name. Observe the flow of the item. Every part is complete in itself with smaller chunks so that it is not overwhelming for the learner.

The factors of 18 in increasing order: 


 1, 2, [3], [6], 9, and [18].


Tval Task:  Your friend says that 3 and 6 and 6 and 3 are two different factor pairs of 18. Is he correct? Why?
ER: No, your friend is not correct.
 Factor pairs are made by multiplying two numbers to get a product.
For 18: 3×6=18 and 6×3=18
These are the same factor pair because the order doesn't matter in multiplication.
So, (3, 6) and (6, 3) are just the same pair written differently.

Tval Task:  
The factor pairs of 16 are (1 and 16), (2 and 8), and (4 and 4). Explain to your teacher how the last factor pair is different from the other pairs of factors. 


Can you think of another number that has a similar type of factor pair?


ER: In the other pairs like (1 and 16) or (2 and 8), the two numbers are different. But in (4 and 4), it’s a repeated factor — this means 4 × 4 = 16, and we only write it once because switching the order doesn’t give a new pair.
Another number with a similar factor pair: 9, because: 3×3=9
	

Hint 1: The two numbers that we multiply to get the product are its factor pairs.
Hint 2: For example, what number do you multiply by 3 to get 12? What number do you multiply by 6 to get 12?

* What do you multiply by 8 to get 16? [2]
* What do you multiply by 4 to get 16? [4]
* Which numbers divide 16 exactly? [1, 2, 4, 8, 16]

Part 3 : Justification: 


This item begins with a simulation - step by step to teach the concept of factors and there is a CFU at the end of the first part after the simulation to reinforce what all factors of 8 are and what is the definition of factor. Student actually arrives at the definition on their own as it is already taught in the simulation. This is followed by another activity using polypad. It touches upon why 0 cannot be a factor before getting into another simulation to show how all factors can be found using factor tree method, followed by some guided and some independent tasks. There are CFUs at the end of every widget. 
Flow : Factors as numbers that divide every number exactly- that justifies why 0 is not a factor, then going ahead to find all factors of a number. It did not start with the multiplication definition because then we would not have been able to justify why 0 is not a factor. So the flow is perfect!
