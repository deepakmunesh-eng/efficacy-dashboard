Learning Item Review
Why this doc exists.  It's a simple, repeatable way to review a learning item. We look at the same five things every time, note what's working and what could be better, and show the screen we're talking about. Use it as a template for the next set of sheets too.
The five things we check
For every learning item, we ask:
1.  Flow  |  Does the lesson move smoothly, and does it finish one idea before starting the next?
2.  Visuals & simulations  |  Are there enough visuals/simulations — and do they actually help, without giving away the answer or showing something misleading?
3.  Text load  |  Is the screen easy to read, or is it too text-heavy?
4.  Response boxes  |  Are there a sensible number of answer boxes in one widget — not too many at once?
5.  Guided example & accuracy  |  Is there a worked example for students to learn from, and is everything on the screen correct?
Grade 3 — Measurement of Mass
Lesson  |  Compare and Order Objects According to Their Masses
Internal ID  |  US-G3-Compare-and-Order-Objects-…-V3-1.W05
Variant  |  Single units — grams only or kilograms only
What this lesson is meant to teach:
·       Compare masses in single units (grams only or kilograms only).
·       Compare masses in compound units (kilograms and grams).
·       Order objects by their masses in single and compound units.
Flow  	● Suggested change
In the story, it reads like the yellow character (Teddy) has the big watermelon and the blue one (Kitty) has the small one — but on the weighing scale the two are swapped. And because one watermelon is clearly bigger, a child can already guess who's heavier without even using the scale, which is the whole point of the lesson. A nicer way: give both characters an identical, closed bag of mixed fruit so size doesn't give it away, then bring in the scale — and keep the same character-to-object pairing from the story right through to 1a and 1b.
Visuals & simulations  	● Working well
There's no simulation here, and honestly it doesn't need one — the images carry the idea nicely on their own.
Text load  	● Working well
Light and easy to read. No changes needed.
Response boxes  	● Working well
A sensible number of boxes — nothing feels crowded.
Guided example & accuracy  	● Suggested change
Good guided examples are already in place — that part is lovely.
One accuracy fix: in 1d the smartphone's mass starts at 100 g, but real phones are about 150–240 g, so the minimum should be 150 g. Also, the characters were renamed from Teddy/Kitty, so please make sure the new names show up everywhere.
Grade 4 — Fractions
Lesson  |  Generate Equivalent Fractions
Internal ID  |  US-G4-Generate-Equivalent-Fractions-V3-1.W02
What this lesson is meant to teach:
·       Make equivalent fractions by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number.
·       Make equivalent fractions by dividing the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number.
Flow  	● Suggested change
1g tries to squeeze two different questions into one widget, and the explanation doesn't quite land. Keep it to one question, and phrase it more directly — something like: “First, find what to multiply the denominator 4 by to get 12:  4 × ___ = 12.”
Visuals & simulations  	● Suggested change
Two things to look at. In 1d the instruction says “without visual models,” but fraction bars are right there on the screen — the words and the picture contradict each other. And in 2d there's an image that isn't really needed and doesn't correctly show the equivalent fraction. Fix: match the wording to what's shown (or drop the bars), and remove/replace the 2d image.
Text load  	● Working well
The amount of text reads fine.
Response boxes  	● Suggested change
1a–1b has 6 response boxes in one widget, and no solutions are provided — that's a lot to fill in with nothing to check against. Trim the number of boxes and give the solutions.
Guided example & accuracy  	● Suggested change
There's no guided example for 1a, so students jump straight into answering — adding one would help. A couple of small task tweaks: 2a would read more clearly with 8/12 and 2/3 instead of 4/6; and since 2b already gives 3/4, just ask what to divide by (2) rather than restating it. Also, 2g doesn't introduce anything new, so it can simply be combined with 2h.
Grade 5 — Fractions: Addition & Subtraction
Lesson  |  Add Fractions with Unlike Denominators
Internal ID  |  US-G5-Add-Fractions-with-Unlike-Denominators-V3-1.W02
What this lesson is meant to teach:
·       Find equivalent fractions with common denominators.
·       Add fractions with different denominators.
·       Add mixed numbers with different denominators.
Flow  	● Suggested change
1b never actually finishes 1/4 + 1/2 — it jumps to a new pair of fractions before reaching the answer, so students don't get to see the addition land. It also runs long. Let 1/4 + 1/2 reach its answer first, then move on, and shorten the widget so each pair wraps up before the next begins.
Visuals & simulations  	● Suggested change
In 1c the strips for 3/4 and 5/6 are drawn at different lengths. So when each is split into 12 parts in 1d, a twelfth of 3/4 ends up looking smaller than a twelfth of 5/6 — which isn't right, because the two wholes should be the same length. Separately, in 2b the two pizzas look different (4 toppings vs. 3) even though they should match. Fix: draw both wholes the same length, and make the pizzas identical.
Text load  	● Suggested change
1b is on the long/heavy side (this ties into the flow point above). Tightening it up will make it much easier to follow.
Response boxes  	● Working well
The number of response boxes is fine.
Guided example & accuracy  	● Suggested change
One accuracy slip in 1d: the text says “let us express 1/4 and 1/6 with a common denominator,” but the fractions actually on screen are 3/4 and 5/6 — that reference needs correcting. A small grammar tidy in 2b too: “1/2 of a pizza … 1/3 of the same pizza” reads better as “1/2 of a pizza” and “1/3 of a pizza.” The rest — 1e, 1f, 1g — all look good.
Grade 6 — Introduction to Algebra & Algebraic Expressions
Lesson  |  Evaluate Algebraic Expressions
Internal ID  |  US-G6-Evaluate-Algebraic-Expressions-V3-1.W02
What this lesson is meant to teach:
·       Evaluate expressions with a single variable.
·       Evaluate expressions with multiple variables.
Flow  	● Working well
The rest of Item 1 flows fine.
Visuals & simulations  	● Suggested change
Just a small cleanup on the 1a screen: an internal label “Expression Evaluation Visual Guide” is showing that shouldn't be there, and “Evaluate” and “b − 5” are stuck together with no space. Remove the label and add the space.
Text load  	● Working well
Reads fine.
Response boxes  	● Working well
A reasonable number of boxes.
Guided example & accuracy  	● Suggested change
The main thing here is keeping the rule name consistent. 1b introduces MDAS, but 1c switches to PEDMAS — and they actually clash: 1b implies multiplication comes first, while 1c says division comes before multiplication. On top of that, P and E are never explained, and it says exponents come before addition without mentioning they also come before multiplication and division. Later on, 2d switches again to PEMDAS. Please pick ONE name, use it everywhere, explain every letter, and state the full order clearly. Two quick assessment fixes: don't ask students to type the words “multiplication” or “exponents” — there are too many ways to spell/write them, so use a dropdown or multiple choice instead.
