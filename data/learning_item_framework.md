# Learning Item Review Framework (V3.1)

Built from the **Gold Standard Items** document. Its four worked examples (Polygons,
Place Value, Factors, Integers) — each with the designer's justification for why it is
strong, plus a "not-so-good" counter-example — give us the principles below. Use this to
review every V3.1 **learning** item and label it **Good / Average / Bad**.

The gold standard rewards one thing above all: **guided discovery** — the student is led
to *arrive at* the idea through small, scaffolded steps, not *told* the idea and then
quizzed once.

---

## The six dimensions (score each 1–5)

### 1. Conceptual flow & sequencing
Does the item build in a deliberate, correct order — **known → unknown, concrete →
abstract**?
- **Good:** Each widget is complete in itself and sets up the next. Order is pedagogically
  deliberate (e.g. Factors defines "divides exactly, no remainder" *first* so that "why 0
  isn't a factor" can be justified, *then* lists factors).
- **Bad:** Jumps straight to the abstract/definition; order forces the student to derive the
  rule with no lead-in; covers only one narrow case and expects generalisation.

### 2. Guided discovery & scaffolding
Does the student *construct* the concept, or just receive it?
- **Good:** Short chunked widgets, a **Check-for-Understanding after each**, hints that
  nudge (not reveal), Teacher-Tips / Cue-Don't-Tell questioning, and "Acknowledgements"
  where the student explains in their own words. Scaffolding matched to the grade.
- **Bad:** Passive telling with a single CFU at the end; no hints/teacher-tips; one big
  overwhelming widget; scaffolding too thin for the grade level.

### 3. Examples & non-examples / guided practice
Are there enough worked + guided examples, and does it dissect what the concept **is and
is not**, before an independent task?
- **Good:** Multiple examples AND non-examples pulled apart with reasons (Polygon: a circle
  is *not* one, a curved shape is *not* one), ending in an independent check.
- **Bad:** No non-examples; no guided task to dissect cases; too few examples before asking
  the student to do it alone.

### 4. Visuals & simulations
Do visuals/manipulatives/simulations actually **teach** the idea (Polypad, number track,
place-value blocks), or are they decoration / mismatched?
- **Good:** The simulation carries a step the words can't; concrete models appear before the
  abstract form.
- **Bad:** No concrete representation where the grade needs it; a visual that contradicts the
  text; a decorative image that adds nothing.

### 5. Text load & language
Is the reading light and age-appropriate?
- **Good:** Short, plain, readable; nothing crowded.
- **Bad:** Text-heavy widgets; difficult words for the grade; long passages that bury the task.

### 6. Response design & accuracy
Are the response boxes sensible in number/placement, and is **everything correct**?
- **Good:** A reasonable number of boxes, nothing crowded; answers, keys, and instructions all
  correct and consistent with what's shown.
- **Bad:** Too many boxes in one widget with no guided example; a wrong answer/key; an
  instruction that contradicts the visual (e.g. "without visual models" while a model is shown).

---

## Rating bands

| Rating | Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Good** | **4.0–5.0** | Guided discovery throughout; correct concrete→abstract flow; examples **and** non-examples; CFUs after each step; visuals teach; light text; correct & well-placed responses. A model item. |
| **Average** | **2.5–3.9** | Teaches the concept correctly, but has gaps: some passive stretches, thin scaffolding, few/no non-examples, or minor text/visual/response issues. No accuracy errors. Works, but wouldn't be shown as an exemplar. |
| **Bad** | **1.0–2.4** | Passive "tell-then-quiz"; missing prior-knowledge or scaffolding; jumps to abstract; no example/non-example dissection; **or** any genuine accuracy error / contradiction. Needs a rebuild. |

Scoring note: an item with an outright **accuracy error or a text-vs-visual contradiction is
capped at Bad**, regardless of how good the rest is — correctness is non-negotiable.

## Worked calibration (from the gold standard)

**GOOD — Grade 3 · "Identify what a Polygon is" (Gold Standard Example 1).**
Starts from prior knowledge (open vs closed shapes) → narrows to closed shapes of only
straight lines → defines *polygon* → CFUs that ask the student to judge a circle and a
mixed-line shape *with reasons* → ends with an independent "which is a polygon?" hotspot.
Concrete, guided, examples + non-examples, CFU after every widget. **≈ 4.8/5.**

**BAD — the same lesson, "not-so-good approach" (Gold Standard Example 1, Part 4).**
Opens by *stating* "a closed shape of only straight lines is a polygon," shows one example,
asks one CFU. No prior-knowledge activation, no non-examples, no dissection — pure passive
telling for a Grade-3 concept that needs scaffolding. **≈ 2.0/5.**

**BAD — Place Value "alternative approach" (Gold Standard Example 2, Part 4).**
Passive explanation, a single CFU, and it only ever touches the **thousands** place —
expecting the student to generalise with no concrete blocks and no scaffolding at Grade 3.
**≈ 2.2/5.**

**AVERAGE — what "in between" looks like** (illustrative, in the same spirit).
An item that teaches the concept correctly and uses a model, but leans on telling for the
first two widgets, offers only one guided example and no non-examples, and puts six response
boxes in one widget before the student has practised. Nothing is *wrong*, but the discovery
and scaffolding the gold standard expects are thin. **≈ 3.2/5.**
